Research Interests

My research explores some of the most energetic environments in the Universe: the regions surrounding supermassive black holes at the centers of galaxies. These objects power Active Galactic Nuclei (AGN), where matter spirals inward and releases enormous amounts of energy before crossing the event horizon. Using X-ray observations from space telescopes such as NuSTAR and XMM-Newton, I investigate how black holes accrete matter, how their hot X-ray emitting coronae are formed, and how powerful winds moving at significant fractions of the speed of light are launched from their vicinity. Through spectral and timing analysis, I aim to uncover the physical processes that govern these extreme systems and shape their observable properties. Beyond black hole astrophysics, I am interested in developing computational and machine-learning techniques for astronomical data analysis, with applications ranging from gamma-ray bursts to other high-energy transient phenomena.

Machine Learning for Gamma-Ray Bursts

Gamma-ray bursts (GRBs) are among the most energetic transient events in the Universe. My research focuses on developing machine-learning and statistical techniques for reconstructing and analyzing GRB light curves. Using Gaussian Processes, Deep Gaussian Processes, and uncertainty-aware models, I investigate the temporal evolution of these high-energy phenomena and extract physical insights from complex astronomical datasets.
